Basic info

Rabbits are small mammals known for their soft fur, long ears, and short, fluffy tails. They are herbivores, feeding mostly on hay, grass, and leafy vegetables. With their powerful back legs, rabbits can hop quickly and cover surprising distances. In the wild, they often live in burrows and are most active at dawn and dusk. Domestic rabbits are usually gentle and quiet, making them great companions for families. With proper care, they can live up to 10 years or more. Rabbits also groom themselves frequently and enjoy having space to explore. Because of their curious nature and calm temperament, they are considered ideal pets for those who can give them time and attention.

Rabbits have excellent hearing and can rotate their long ears to detect sounds from different directions. This helps them stay alert in the wild and avoid predators. Their teeth never stop growing, so they need to chew on hay or wood to keep them at a healthy length. Rabbits are highly social animals and often thrive best when kept in pairs. They communicate through body language, such as thumping their feet or flicking their ears, to show fear or excitement. A rabbit that lies down with its legs stretched out is usually feeling very relaxed. If a rabbit nudges your hand or gently bumps its head against you, it may be asking for your attention or affection. Their behavior is often subtle but can be quite expressive once you learn to understand it. Providing a safe, quiet, and enriching environment is key to keeping your rabbit happy and healthy.

A rabbit’s ears are not just for hearing — they also help regulate body temperature. When it’s hot, the blood vessels in their large ears help release excess heat. This natural cooling system is one reason rabbits thrive in many climates. Their ears can even rotate independently to better detect sounds from all directions, making them very alert animals.

Rabbits have powerful hind legs built for jumping and running. In the wild, these legs allow them to escape predators quickly by darting in unpredictable directions. Their strong legs are also great for hopping over obstacles and digging. Domestic rabbits still have this instinct and may thump their feet as a warning signal when they sense danger.

When rabbits feel safe and content, they may flop onto their side — a behavior known as a “bunny flop.” This action shows complete trust and comfort. Rabbits also perform a move called a "binky," where they jump and twist in the air. It’s their way of expressing pure joy. A rabbit that shows these signs is healthy, happy, and well cared for.

Why rabbits make great pets

  1. Quiet and clean: Rabbits are silent pets and often keep themselves tidy.
  2. Social: They bond with owners and may gently ask for attention.
  3. Playful: They enjoy toys and hopping around, making them fun and engaging companions!
